Picking the right tags for the job

A primer on HTML semantics and element choice

What does it mean for HTML to be semantic?

Being semantic means using HTML to represent the structure and content of your page irrespective of appearance or behavior. Think of your tags as labels describing the various elements of the document.

Why does it matter?

For the same reasons it matters to follow the conventions of “real” programming languages. Being semantic with your HTML ensures that you—and others—will get the most out of your markup. HTML is not a very strict language (you can thank its XML roots for that) so being semantic is a good way to ensure proper usage, especially since it won't yell at you if you're wrong.

Don't forget about accessibility.

Writing semantic HTML automatically improves your site's accessibility. Users who visit your site using screen readers will appreciate the better experience. UX isn't just for the sighted. There's a lot more that goes into building very accessible pages than just being semantic. If you're interested in making your web sites as accessible as possible W3C's accessibility section is a good place to start.

<blockquote>

Use a <blockquote> when you are including a quotation from another source that isn't inline—a passage rather than a phrase, for example. If you're quoting a source inline, you should use <q>. You may also optionally include a “cite” attribute to reference the source (it isn't displayed by default, though).

            
<blockquote cite="http://www.perseus.tufts.edu/hopper/text?doc=Perseus%3Atext%3A1999.01.0126%3Abook%3D1%3Achapter%3D133%3Asection%3D3">
Moreover, it is their custom to deliberate about the gravest matters when they are drunk; and what they approve in their deliberations is proposed to them the next day, when they are sober, by the master of the house where they deliberate; and if, being sober, they still approve it, they act on it, but if not, they drop it. And if they have deliberated about a matter when sober, they decide upon it when they are drunk.
</blockquote>

<fieldset>

The fieldset allows you to group similar sections of forms. For example, if you have a registration form, you could wrap log in fields and user location fields in <fieldsets> so they are separate at the document level. If you've been doing this with <div>s, swap them out for these.

            
<form action="" method="POST">

  <fieldset>
    <label for="username">Username</label>
    <input id="username" type="text" name="username">
  </fieldset>

  <fieldset>
    <label for="address">Address</label>
    <input id="address" type="text" name="address">

    <label for="city">City</label>
    <input id="city" type="text" name="city">

    <label for="state">State</label>
    <input id="state" type="text" name="state">

    <label for="zip">Zip</label>
    <input id="zip" type="text" name="zip">
  </fieldset>

</form>

<header>

The <header> is meant to group introductory or navigational aids. For example you may want to have a heading, table of contents, search, logo, etc. at the beginning of a section—sticking those in a <header> would be ideal.

  • Block
  • Inline

<footer>

<footer> is very similar to <header> but for information you may want to include at the end of a section such as author(s)/attribution, copyright, links to related content, etc.

  • Block
  • Inline

<section>

The <section> tag might look like the semantic equivalent of a div but it most certainly is not. The <section> tag is used to define a group of related content in your document (usually with a heading)—think of it like a chapter in a book. If you're looking to wrap content in a block level element just for styling, use a div. If that content thematically belongs together as a group in your document, then this might be the tag you want.

  • Block
  • Inline

<article>

Of all the semantic HTML5 tags, I find this one to be the least clear. In practice it's not tricky to use. The easiest way to think about the <article> tag is to scrutinize your content. Is this content that can be syndicated as is? If whatever is in that <article> is self contained and could be shared as a post on a blog, a comment, or an item that could appear in an RSS feed, then you should use this tag. If you're looking to just group thematically similar content that would be missing context on its own, then you probably want the <section> tag above.

  • Block
  • Inline

<aside>

The <aside> is for supplementary content. When used outside of an <article>, it should contain content related to the site itself—more nav, links to related content on the site or elsewhere, related ads, etc. When used inside of an <article>, the content in the <aside> should be related to the <article> itself—perhaps a pullquote. The <aside> is not for content that is simply meant to be adjacent to the main content.

block vs inline

HTML5 does away with the block/inline nomenclature and introduces a handful of different content categories—Metadata, Flow, Sectioning, Heading, Phrasing, Embedded, Interactive, and Form-associated. The elements in these new categories still behave like block or inline elements, though. Generally, block level content's closest analog is Flow content and inline's is Phrasing content.

block level elements…

break up the flow of a document. They cannot be placed next to other block level elements and create new lines above and below themselves.

they...

  • can be nested in other block elements
  • can be given an explicit width/height
  • can be floated*
  • can be positioned absolutely*
  • should not be nested in inline elements

inline level elements…

do not break up the flow of a document. They can be placed next to other inline level elements and will not interrupt the flow inside of a block level parent.

they...

  • can be nested in block or inline elements
  • cannot be given an explicit width/height
  • cannot be floated*
  • cannot be positioned absolutely*
  • should not wrap block elements

* block level elements are the only elements that can be floated or positioned absolutely. The moment you apply either CSS property to an inline element, it immediately becomes a block level element. Surprise!

With that in mind, let's talk about <div>s and <spans>s

<div>s and <spans>s have no semantic value. Zero. None. They are generic wrappers—block (or flow) and inline (or phrasing), respectively—for your CSS to take advantage of. Use a <div> when you want a block level wrapper; use a <span> when you want an inline wrapper. If your content has semantic meaning, use the appropriate document level tag for that content instead of one of these generic wrappers.
